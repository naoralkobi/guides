How to Actually Use Claude: 18 Steps

AI · Productivity · Setup + copy-paste prompts

The Big Idea

Most people who use Claude every day are still using about 10% of what it can do — not because it's complicated, but because nobody showed them the other 90%. These 18 steps set Claude up to remember you, understand you, and work the way you actually think.

💡 The actual point

Claude isn't smarter than you and doesn't have better ideas. What it has is infinite patience, broad knowledge, and the ability to attack a problem from angles you haven't considered. The people who get the most from it aren't the ones with the best questions — they're the ones who set it up to understand them and use it as a partner, not a dispenser. Set it up once; change how you work permanently.

Start Here: Set Up Memory

1Create a Project, not a chat

Every new chat starts with zero memory — it doesn't know your name, work, goals, or how you communicate, so you waste the first few messages re-explaining yourself. A Project is a persistent workspace where Claude keeps context across every conversation inside it. In Claude, click Projects in the sidebar, create one, and name it something like "Work" or "Personal." Everything that follows goes inside it.

2Tell Claude who you are

Paste this into your Project and fill in every field honestly — the more specific you are, the better every response becomes. Save it in the Project's knowledge base so Claude reads it at the start of every conversation.

prompt 
My name is [your name].

I work as [your role or profession]. My main responsibilities are
[2-3 things you actually do day to day].

Right now my biggest goals are [1-3 specific goals you're working toward].

I use Claude mostly for [writing, research, analysis, learning, coding, etc].

My background and knowledge level: [what you know well, what you're
learning, what you're new to].

How I like to receive information: [e.g. direct and concise / detailed
with examples / step by step / no bullet points / short paragraphs].

Things I don't want: [e.g. don't add disclaimers, don't use corporate
language, don't repeat what I just said back to me, don't start with
"Great question"].

Topics and areas I care about: [your interests, industry, niche].

3Turn that into Custom Instructions

Background tells Claude who you are; Custom Instructions tell it how to behave by default. After filling in the template above, run this and paste the output into your Project Instructions.

prompt 
Based on everything I've told you about myself, write me a set of custom
instructions for this Claude Project.

The instructions should:
- Describe who I am and what I do
- Set my default communication style and format
- Tell Claude what to never do when working with me
- Define the tone I want in every response
- Include any default behaviors I would want in every session

Write them in second person, as if Claude is reading rules about how to
help me. Be specific. No generic advice. Under 400 words.

Claude Is Not What You Think

4Claude is not a search engine

Treating Claude like Google — type a question, wait for an answer — is the lowest-value way to use it and cuts its usefulness by ~80%. It's a thinking partner that reasons, synthesizes, and argues. Stop asking what something is; start asking it to think through something with you.

Instead ofTry
"What is prompt caching?""I'm building a workflow that calls Claude 20 times per session. Walk me through how prompt caching works and whether it would actually reduce my costs given that context."

5Ask Claude to ask you questions first

Before any complex task, have Claude gather information from you — the output is dramatically better when built on the right foundation instead of assumptions you then have to correct.

prompt 
Before you start, ask me the 5 most important questions that would help
you do this well.

After I answer, then begin.

What Even Regular Users Don't Know

6Style cloning

Without examples, Claude writes in its own voice — correct and completely wrong in tone. Give it 3–5 samples of your writing and ask it to analyze your patterns, not just describe your style.

prompt 
Here are 3 examples of my writing:

[paste sample 1]
[paste sample 2]
[paste sample 3]

Analyze my writing style in detail. Look at: sentence length, rhythm,
vocabulary choices, how I open and close paragraphs, what I avoid, how
formal or informal I am, and any patterns that make my writing distinct.

After this, when I ask you to write anything for me, match this style
exactly. Do not default to your own patterns.

7Claude as your sparring partner

Asking Claude to "help with" an idea gets you agreement and elaboration. To stress-test it, tell Claude to attack it — not critique it. The distinction matters.

prompt 
Here is my plan: [describe your plan]

Your job is to destroy it. Find every assumption I'm making that could
be wrong. Find every way this could fail. Argue the opposite position as
hard as you can. Do not be polite. Do not add qualifications. Just attack.

After that, steelman my position. Build the strongest possible case for
why I'm right.

Then tell me what you actually think.

8Extended Thinking

Extended Thinking makes Claude reason step by step before answering. Skip it for simple tasks; turn it on for complex decisions and analysis. Click the brain icon before sending, or add:

prompt 
Think through this carefully before responding.

Work through the problem step by step, show your reasoning, identify
where you're uncertain, then give me your conclusion.

9Claude writes prompts for Claude

If you're not sure how to prompt for a task, ask Claude to write the prompt — it knows what instructions produce better results.

prompt 
I need Claude to help me [describe your actual task].

Write me the best possible prompt for this task.

Include role, context, format instructions, and any constraints that
would improve the output.

Then use that prompt immediately.

Spend Fewer Tokens, Get More

10Specify the output length

Claude defaults to writing more than you need. Telling it the length up front cuts token usage on most tasks by 40–60% with no loss of value.

prompt 
Answer in 3 sentences maximum.
Give me 5 bullet points. No explanations. Just the points.
Write this in under 150 words.

11Remove the preamble

"Great question. Let me break this down…", restatements, disclaimers, closing summaries — you didn't ask for any of it, and it costs tokens and time. Add this to your Custom Instructions:

prompt 
Never start responses with preamble, affirmations, or restatements of
my question.

Go directly to the answer.

Do not add a summary at the end unless I specifically ask for one.

No disclaimers unless the topic genuinely requires one.

12Don't re-explain yourself every conversation

Pasting the same background into every new chat wastes tokens every time. That's what Projects and Custom Instructions are for: put your context in once, let Claude read it automatically, never paste it again. If you're not using Projects yet, start there before anything else.

13Start a new chat for a new topic

Claude carries everything said earlier in a conversation. Switching topics mid-chat means more tokens per response, slower processing, and context bleed. For something unrelated, start a fresh chat inside your Project — you keep the Project memory and drop the irrelevant baggage.

⚠️ Token discipline, in one line

Set length, kill preamble, store context once, and start fresh chats per topic. These four habits compound — and they save the most as your Claude usage scales.

Ready-to-Use Prompts

14Understand anything via analogies (Feynman method)

Use case: real comprehension, not a definition you can't actually use.

prompt 
Explain [topic] to me using only analogies and everyday examples. No
jargon. Assume I have no background in this field.

After each analogy, check whether I've actually understood it by asking
me one question. Based on my answer, go deeper or adjust the explanation.

Keep going until I can explain it back to you in my own words without
using any technical terms.

15Travel plan built around how you actually travel

Use case: an itinerary for your style, not a generic must-see list.

prompt 
I'm planning a trip to [destination]. I'll be there for [number of days].
My budget is approximately [amount] per day including accommodation.

Here's how I actually travel: [slow mornings or packed days, touristy
places or local spots, museums or food, active or relaxed, solo or with
someone, etc].

Things I want to avoid: [crowds, tourist traps, expensive restaurants,
long transport times, etc].

Build me a day-by-day itinerary that fits this. For each day, include
where to stay, what to do, where to eat, and any logistics I need to
know. Flag anything that requires booking in advance.

16Monthly expense analysis with real conclusions

Use case: turn a bank statement into a clear picture and one high-impact change. Works best with real data.

prompt 
I'm going to paste my expenses from the last month. Analyze them and
tell me:

1. What categories am I spending the most on
2. Where my spending looks unusual compared to my stated goals
3. What I could cut without meaningfully affecting my life
4. What I'm probably underspending on that matters
5. One specific change that would have the biggest financial impact

Here are my expenses: [paste your bank statement or expense list]

My financial goals right now: [save more, pay off debt, invest, etc]

17Claude as your personal thinking partner

Use case: structured self-reflection with an outside perspective. Not therapy.

prompt 
I want to talk through something I'm dealing with. Your job is not to
give me advice right away.

First, ask me questions to understand the situation fully. What's
actually happening, how I feel about it, what I've already tried, and
what outcome I'm hoping for.

After you understand the full picture, reflect back what you're hearing
— not just the facts but what seems to be underneath them.

Then, and only then, offer your perspective. Be honest, not reassuring.
Tell me what you actually think, including anything I might not want to
hear.

Here's what's on my mind: [describe what you want to think through]

18Stress-test a business idea before you commit

Use case: a ruthless first filter that finds real problems before they cost you time and money.

prompt 
I have a business idea I want to stress-test before I invest serious
time in it.

Here's the idea: [what it is, who it's for, how it makes money, why you
think it works]

Your job is to find everything wrong with it. Specifically:

1. What assumptions am I making that could be wrong
2. Who already does this and why I might lose to them
3. Why the target customer might not actually pay for this
4. What would have to be true for this to work, and how likely is that
5. The single biggest problem with this idea

Be specific. Generic risks like "the market might not be ready" are not
useful. Give me the real version of each problem.

After that, tell me what the idea would need to look like to actually work.
